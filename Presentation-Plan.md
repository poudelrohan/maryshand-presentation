# Mary's Hand — 5-Minute Pitch Presentation Plan

> **Format:** 3-min pre-recorded video plays first, then 5-min live presentation (with Q&A integrated)
> **Goal:** Score 4/4 on every rubric category

---

## Rubric Alignment Map

| Rubric Category | What Gets 4 Points | Which Slides Cover It |
|-----------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| **App Relevance** | Addresses the challenge statement, will make impact on campus AND surrounding community | Slides 1-3 |
| **Pitch Content** | Persuasive pitch backed by evidence, shows how app meets/exceeds/redefines user needs | Slides 3-5 |
| **Pitch Delivery** | Creative memorable storytelling, engaging visuals, smooth transitions between team members | All slides (delivery style) |
| **User Interface** | Design empowers users, uses animation, color, layout for seamless engaging experience | Slide 6 (Live Demo) |
| **User Experience** | Innovating, surpassing, delightful; sets it apart from competitors | Slides 6-7 |
| **Accessibility** | Speech recognition, screen reading, alternative input, high contrast, screen magnification | Slide 8 |

## Slide-by-Slide Boilerplate

### SLIDE 1 — Title (15 seconds)
**On Screen:**
- App name: "Mary's Hand"
- Tagline: "Your community, always within reach."
- BCU logo / school name
- Team member names

**What to SAY:**
> "Good morning everyone. We are [names] from Bethune-Cookman University, and today we're presenting Mary's Hand — a community app that puts food, events, and AI-powered assistance right in your hands."

**Rubric target:** Pitch Delivery (strong opening, introduce all team members)

---

### SLIDE 2 — The Problem (45 seconds)
**On Screen:**
- Stat: "1 in 3 college students face food insecurity" (cite source)
- Volusia County / Daytona Beach food desert data
- A visual map or graphic showing food deserts near BCU
- Stat on community disconnection among students

**What to SAY:**
> "At BCU, food insecurity isn't just a statistic — it's a daily reality. 1 in 3 college students skip meals. In Volusia County, many neighborhoods around our campus are classified as food deserts. But the problem goes deeper — students also feel disconnected from the community resources that exist right around them. We asked: what if technology could bridge that gap?"

**Rubric target:** App Relevance (impact on campus AND surrounding community), Pitch Content (problem backed by evidence)

---

### SLIDE 3 — Our Research / Challenge-Based Learning (45 seconds)
**On Screen:**
- Challenge-Based Learning framework diagram (Big Idea -> Essential Question -> Challenge)
- Big Idea: Community Well-Being
- Essential Question: "How can we use technology to reduce food insecurity and strengthen community bonds at BCU?"
- Challenge: "Design an app that connects students to surplus food, volunteer opportunities, and AI meal assistance"
- Data sources: USDA food atlas, campus surveys, local food bank partnerships

**What to SAY:**
> "We used the Challenge-Based Learning model. Our big idea was community well-being. Our essential question: how can technology reduce food insecurity and strengthen community bonds right here at BCU? Through research — USDA data, campus surveys, and conversations with local organizations — we discovered that food, events, and knowledge were the three pillars we needed to connect."

**Rubric target:** App Relevance (research-backed), Pitch Content (evidence-based approach)

---

### SLIDE 4 — The Solution: Three Pillars (30 seconds)
**On Screen:**
- App icon centered
- Three pillars displayed visually:
  1. **Food Sharing** — Share & claim surplus food via map
  2. **Community Events** — Volunteer events from university & local orgs
  3. **Mary's Kitchen AI** — Meal planning, storage tips, rationing help
- "From her hand to yours" legacy line connecting to Mary McLeod Bethune

**What to SAY:**
> "Mary's Hand is built on three pillars. First, food sharing — a map-based system where anyone can share surplus food and others can claim it. Second, community events — connecting students with volunteer opportunities on campus and in Daytona Beach. Third, Mary's Kitchen AI — an AI assistant that helps with meal planning, food storage, and making the most of what you have. The name honors Mary McLeod Bethune — from her hand to yours."

**Rubric target:** Pitch Content (clear purpose, target audience, how it meets user needs)

---

### SLIDE 5 — How It Works (30 seconds)
**On Screen:**
- Quick visual flow: Open app -> See food on map -> Claim it -> Get directions
- Or: Post surplus food -> Pin location -> Community benefits
- Or: Ask Mary's AI -> Get meal plan -> Save food from waste

**What to SAY:**
> "Here's how it works. Open the app, and you'll see surplus food pinned on a map around Daytona Beach. Tap to claim, and a 2.5-second confirmation bar prevents accidental taps — inspired by Uber Eats. Once claimed, you get directions. You can also donate food: snap a photo, drop a pin, and post. And Mary's AI can help you plan meals with whatever you already have."

**Rubric target:** User Experience (intuitive flow explanation before demo)

---

### SLIDE 6 — Live Demo (90 seconds) **[LONGEST SECTION]**
**On Screen:**
- Live app running on device or simulator (screen shared)
- Walk through each feature in order:
  1. Map view with food pins
  2. Claim flow with confirmation bar
  3. Donate food flow
  4. Mary's Kitchen AI chat
  5. Volunteer events + sign up
  6. Quick flash of settings/accessibility

**What to SAY:**
> [Walk through each feature naturally, narrating what you're doing]
> "Let me show you the app live. Here's the food map — you can see pins from across the Daytona Beach area. I'll tap this one... see the details and photo. Now I'll claim it — watch the confirmation bar fill up to prevent accidental claims. Claimed. Now I can get directions."
> "If I want to donate, I tap the plus button, take a photo, drop a pin on the map, and post."
> "Here's Mary's AI — I can ask something like 'what can I make with rice and beans?' and she gives me a full meal plan."
> "And here are community volunteer events — I can browse, sign up with the same confirmation bar, and get directions."

**Rubric target:** User Interface (show animations, color, layout), User Experience (demonstrate the flow), Pitch Delivery (visuals supporting the story)

### SLIDE 8 — Accessibility (30 seconds)
**On Screen:**
- "18+ Accessibility Features" as headline
- Grid or list of key features:
  - OpenDyslexic font support
  - Full VoiceOver support (30+ labels, hints, traits)
  - Dynamic Type scaling
  - Dark mode / Light mode / System matching
  - Haptic feedback
  - 44pt minimum touch targets
  - Confirmation bar (prevents accidental actions)
  - Text-to-speech on AI responses
  - Reduced motion support

**What to SAY:**
> "Accessibility was not an afterthought — it's core to Mary's Hand. We built 18-plus accessibility features. Full VoiceOver support with labels and hints on every screen. OpenDyslexic font for users with dyslexia. Dynamic Type scaling. Dark mode. 44-point minimum touch targets. And our confirmation bar prevents accidental claims — critical for users with motor difficulties. Every user deserves equal access."

**Rubric target:** Accessibility (ALL criteria — speech recognition, screen reading, alternative input, high contrast, screen magnification)

## Delivery Tips (for 4/4 on Pitch Delivery)

- **Multiple presenters**: Split slides between team members. Smooth handoffs.
- **Storytelling**: Start with the problem emotionally (slide 2), then back it up with data (slide 3).
- **Visuals**: Every slide should have visuals, not walls of text. The slides support you, not replace you.
- **Demo**: Practice the demo path so it's smooth. Know exactly what to tap and when.
- **Confidence**: Make eye contact with camera. Speak clearly. Be enthusiastic but genuine.
- **Transitions**: Use phrases like "And this brings us to..." or "Let me show you..." to bridge slides.
